Questions and Answers

Is Kintsugi difficult to learn?

The basic technique can be learned in a single workshop session. Traditional Hon-Kintsugi with urushi lacquer requires more practice — especially managing the humidity-dependent curing process — but modern eco-Kintsugi with resin adhesives is accessible to complete beginners within a day.

What materials do I need to get started?

For eco-Kintsugi: a two-part epoxy resin, gold mica powder, nitrile gloves, soft brushes, and a broken ceramic piece. For traditional Kintsugi: urushi lacquer, gold powder (24K), wet sandpaper, a humidity chamber, and patience. Full kits for both methods are available online.

Can I practise Kintsugi at home?

Yes, especially with eco-friendly kits. Traditional urushi-based Kintsugi is more challenging at home — it requires humidity control and careful handling of the raw lacquer (urushiol allergy risk). Eco-resin methods are safe, affordable, and produce beautiful results.

Is urushi allergy dangerous?

Raw urushi contains urushiol, which causes contact dermatitis similar to poison ivy — itching, redness, blistering. In rare cases, reactions can be severe. Always wear nitrile gloves and work in a ventilated space. Fully cured urushi is inert and considered safe. If you have a known sensitivity to poison ivy or oak, use modern alternatives.

Are Kintsugi-repaired vessels food-safe?

Fully cured traditional urushi is generally considered food-safe. However, the repaired seam is fragile and should not be exposed to high heat (dishwasher, microwave) or acidic foods for extended periods. Eco-resin repairs depend on the specific product — check the manufacturer's food-safety rating.

What materials can be repaired with Kintsugi?

Primarily ceramic, porcelain, and stoneware. The technique can also be applied to glass and some stone materials. It is not suitable for metal, wood, or plastic. The object must be breakable — and worth the effort of repair.

How long does a Kintsugi piece take to complete?

Traditional Hon-Kintsugi: several weeks to months, as each urushi layer requires 1–7 days to cure. Eco-Kintsugi: typically 24–72 hours total, including adhesive curing and finishing. The timeline depends on how many breaks are being repaired and how complex the joins are.

Are there Kintsugi workshops in Turkey?

Yes. Lotus5 Studio in Istanbul, led by Özlem Kevser Birkan, offers both traditional and eco-Kintsugi workshops in Turkish and English — in-person and online. She is a pioneer in bringing authentic Kintsugi practice to Turkey.
