Technique & Materials

The Ancient Six-Step Process

Traditional Method

Hon-Kintsugi: The Authentic Process

The traditional method — Hon-Kintsugi — uses urushi lacquer and pure 24K gold powder. The process requires patience: each layer must cure before the next can be applied.

1

Clean

Remove all debris, dust, and loose fragments from broken surfaces. The join must be clean for the lacquer to bond properly.

2

Adhere

Apply urushi lacquer to the broken edges and press the pieces together. Precise alignment is essential.

3

Cure

Place the joined piece in a humidity chamber at 70–85% relative humidity and 20–25°C. Urushi cures through oxidative polymerization — it requires moisture, not drying.

4

Sand

Once the lacquer has fully cured, carefully sand the surface of the joins using progressively finer wet sandpaper. This reveals the smooth seam beneath.

5

Apply Gold

Brush a final thin layer of urushi over the seam while it is still tacky, then dust 24K gold powder (Maki-e technique) onto it using a soft brush.

6

Polish

After the final cure, burnish the gold seams gently. The golden joins are now the most visible — and most beautiful — part of the object.

Alternatives

Traditional vs. Modern Methods

Traditional (Hon-Kintsugi)

  • Urushi lacquer — plant-derived, durable
  • 24K gold, silver, or platinum powder
  • Weeks to months per piece
  • Requires humidity-controlled curing
  • Highest durability — lasts centuries
  • Potential urushi allergy risk

Modern / Eco Alternatives

  • Epoxy resin or food-safe glue
  • Mica powder mixed with gold paint
  • Hours to days per piece
  • Room-temperature curing
  • Good durability — decorative use
  • Generally hypoallergenic

⚠ Urushi Allergy Warning

Urushi (raw lacquer) contains urushiol — the same compound found in poison ivy. In uncured form it can cause severe contact dermatitis. Wear nitrile gloves and work in a well-ventilated space. Fully cured urushi is considered safe.
