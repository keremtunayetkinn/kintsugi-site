Kintsugi Today

A Global Practice of Repair

Global Reach

From Japan to the World

What began as a Japanese court art has become a worldwide practice. Kintsugi communities exist in Japan, South Korea, Turkey, the United Kingdom, the United States, and Australia.

Popular Culture

Kintsugi Beyond Ceramics

🧠

Psychotherapy

Therapists have adopted Kintsugi as a metaphor for trauma recovery — the idea that healing does not mean returning to who you were before, but becoming stronger at the broken places.

💍

Jewellery & Design

The aesthetic of gold-veined ceramics has influenced jewellery design, fashion textiles, and interior design worldwide.

📱

Social Media

The #kintsugi hashtag has millions of posts on Instagram — the image of golden cracks has become one of the most recognisable symbols of resilience in digital culture.

Eco-Kintsugi

Repair Culture & Sustainability

Traditional Kintsugi is inherently sustainable — it extends the life of an object rather than discarding it. Modern eco-Kintsugi practitioners go further: replacing urushi with plant-based or mineral adhesives, using mica instead of mined gold, and advocating for repair culture as a response to consumer waste. The principle is unchanged: a broken thing, thoughtfully mended, becomes more valuable than it was.
