History & Origins

Six Centuries of Golden Repair

The Origin Story

Ashikaga's Broken Teapot

The story of Kintsugi begins around 1467, during the rule of Ashikaga Yoshimasa, the eighth shogun of the Ashikaga shogunate. His prized Chinese teapot broke, and he sent it back to China for repair. It returned held together with unsightly metal staples. Dissatisfied, the shogun tasked Japanese craftsmen with finding a more beautiful solution. The craftsmen responded with lacquer and gold — and Kintsugi was born.

Timeline

A Chronology of Golden Repair

~14,000 BCE

Jomon Period

Urushi lacquer first used in Japan — the raw material that would one day make Kintsugi possible.

15th Century

Kintsugi Emerges

Born from Shogun Ashikaga Yoshimasa's court. The repair of a Chinese teapot sparks a new art form.

16–17th Century

Tea Ceremony Era

Kintsugi spreads through Chado. Wabi-Sabi aesthetics embrace visible repair as beauty.

20th Century

International Recognition

Western collectors and museums begin acquiring Kintsugi pieces. The philosophy resonates beyond ceramics.

Today

A Global Movement

Kintsugi has become a metaphor for resilience worldwide — applied in art therapy, philosophy, and design.

Cultural Context

Kintsugi and the Way of Tea

Kintsugi did not exist in isolation. It grew within the rich ecosystem of Japanese Chado — the Way of Tea — a practice that elevated every object in the tea room to spiritual significance. Tea masters like Sen no Rikyū championed Wabi-Sabi aesthetics: they preferred the imperfect, the weathered, the repaired over the pristine and new. A bowl repaired with gold carried more character, more history, more soul than an unbroken one. Kintsugi was not a compromise — it was an elevation.
